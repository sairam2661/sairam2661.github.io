## Who’s Really in Control?

When you performed poorly in an offline test a few months ago, did you blame yourself or the difficulty of the paper? When facing challenges in daily life, do you feel the outcome is entirely in your hands, or is it largely influenced by external forces such as teammates, fate, luck, and so on? In essence, the question is: who do you believe has authority over your life—**you** or **someone else**?

> "Whether you think you can, or you think you can’t—you’re right."  
> — _Henry Ford_

### Introducing the Locus of Control (LOC)

This system of belief is known as the **Locus of Control (LOC)**. It reflects the extent to which individuals believe they control the events shaping their lives, represented by a spectrum that ranges from _external_ to _internal_:

- **Internal LOC**: Believing that your actions guide your behavior and outcomes.
- **External LOC**: Believing that your behavior is guided by external factors such as coworkers, fate, or luck.

Interestingly, studies show that people with an **internal LOC** experience greater satisfaction and success across most areas of life (though with an exception: in nursing homes, those with an internal LOC often struggle more with feelings of control).

### My Journey from External to Internal LOC

I used to have an external LOC, often blaming outside causes for the things that happened to me—using it as a defense mechanism of sorts. Blaming something else for my misfortunes felt easier than looking inward, accepting responsibility, and finding ways to improve.

While this mindset allowed for a more laid-back approach to life, I eventually realized I wanted to grow and improve. The LOC is not fixed; it’s a spectrum, meaning that we can shift along it. However, since LOC comprises learned behaviors developed over time, it takes determination to make lasting changes.

### Steps I Took to Shift Towards an Internal LOC

My first step was to **accept myself as I was**—acknowledging that I wasn’t yet at my desired destination, but that was okay. I began to see my actions as determining the impact I bring into the world, whether good or bad. For every situation I’d previously attributed to external forces, I redirected responsibility inward.

For those interested in making a similar shift, here’s what I found helpful:

- **Do your best** in any situation and connect your good actions to yourself.
- View everything else, including mistakes, as instances where _"sometimes things just go wrong."_

It’s essential to remember that chaos is the universe’s fundamental state. Like us, it can bring good or evil into the world, and not everything is within our control.

### Results of Leaning Towards an Internal LOC

Eventually, I found myself more aligned with an internal LOC, feeling more responsible, accountable, determined, and in control. For example, the next time I performed poorly in an exam, I didn’t fall back on blaming external factors. Instead, I analyzed my mistakes through my new lens and aimed to do better.

### In Summary

The LOC framework offers a simplistic approach to understanding and categorizing oneself—whether external, internal, or a blend of both. Everyone has their own approach to life, and each environment may favor a different orientation. But for those unsure of which way to lean, I believe an internal LOC is the way forward.

---

By understanding and shifting our LOC, we can shape how we approach life's challenges and, ultimately, our own growth.
